Case studies.

Two shipped. One in flight. Same operating system, different products.
WhatsNextB2C · Marketplace
Build & Launch · 2025
Context

An event listing and management platform for hyper-local communities in the U.S., starting with Oceanside. Two-sided marketplace: local businesses on one side, residents looking for things to do on the other. Mobile-first audience, weak existing alternatives in the niche.

Problem

Empty marketplace, zero brand. Needed both sides of the network at the same time — no businesses means no events to attend; no audience means no reason for businesses to list. A cold-start problem disguised as a launch.

Approach

Built the platform end-to-end and the GTM around it: B2B sales materials to close local businesses, an onboarding workflow that got listings live in minutes, a lifecycle email sequence keyed to listing milestones, and an SEO framework targeting local-intent queries. Closed the loop with analytics so each cohort informed the next.

Outcome

The two-sided launch worked. Businesses signed up faster than the team could onboard them, and a premium tier (promotions + verification) opened a clear monetization path within the first month.

Next Level AgencyB2B SaaS
Lifecycle & Automation · 2025
Context

A B2B agency-tech business running on GoHighLevel across multiple sub-accounts. Trial users were leaking, payment failures were silent, and the lifecycle was held together by manual ops.

Problem

No systematic way to convert trials, recover failed payments, or reach trial-expiring users at the right moment. Every recovery was a fire drill, every conversion was luck.

Approach

Designed and shipped end-to-end automations across two GHL sub-accounts: pipeline triggers, webhook-based contact syncing, product tagging, trial-expiration reminders, extension workflows, and dunning for failed payments — Make.com handling the integration layer.

Outcome

Trial-to-paid became a system, not a wish. Failed payments routed automatically into a recovery sequence before they became churn. The team stopped firefighting and started compounding.

The short version.

Six years, several disguises, one direction.

For six years I wore a lot of hats across B2B and B2C startups — social media manager, graphics designer, community manager, virtual assistant, business development analyst. The roles changed; the pattern didn't. I kept ending up downstream of decisions someone else had already framed wrong — fixing the surface of a problem that started with positioning.

Product Marketing was the first role where business, technology, and user experience stopped being three different jobs and became one. Positioning a product is product work. Onboarding is marketing work. Retention is both. I stopped trying to choose.

The proof is in the work above: warehouse SaaS for FMCG (Zulaiy / Zbacus), trial-to-paid automation for an agency-tech business (NLA), a two-sided local-events marketplace built and launched solo (WhatsNext), and a consumer product currently in R&D with a live waitlist (Project Chronicle).

What I do now: turn AI products into things people actually use, repeatedly, and tell their friends about.
